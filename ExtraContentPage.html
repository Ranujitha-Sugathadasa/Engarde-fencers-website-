Fencing Weapons.

The three weapons used in fencing are the foil, sabre, and epee. It is generally simple to move between the epee, foil, and sabre because all weapons are based on the same basic set of rules. But, each weapon has an own set of rules that affect the the speed, duration and style of a fencing match.

Swords

Foil

In foil, hits must be made with the weapon's point and are only legal if they connect with the fencing mask's lamé or conductive portion, which together cover the body's trunk, groin, and portions of the neck. A pressure-sensitive button on the foil's tip activates when 500 grams or more of pressure are applied.

The hit is genuine if the tip makes contact with the lamé; if it makes contact with the nonconductive jacket or underwear, the hit is "off-target" and not recorded. After one of the fencers scores an on-target hit, the other fencer has 300 milliseconds to follow suit before the scoring box locks them out. Both on- and off-target hits stop the fencing, but only on-target hits can result in touches. When both fencers make accurate shots, the right-of-way rules are applied to decide who has priority.

Epee

Hits are made with the point in épée, just like in foil, but unlike foil, the target area in épée is the entire body. The scoring box will register a hit when the pressure-sensitive tip of the épée is pressed on any surface without even a connection to the box with a force greater than 750 grams.

The piste is attached to the scoring box by a grounding wire, preventing hits to the piste (fencing area) from being counted as touches. Hits to the piste simply do not register, therefore there is no off-target hit. When one fencer scores a hit, the other fencer has only 40 milliseconds to react before the scoring box locks them out. There is no right-of-way if both fencers score hits; instead, both are awarded touches.

Sabre

There is no minimum pressure limit in sabre, unlike épée and foil, and one can strike with any part of the blade (the tip of the blade is simply rounded and not pressure-sensitive). Sabre is hence distinguished by cutting rather than tip thrusts. Sabreists dress in a lamé that totally covers their jacket, conductive gloves, and conductive masks. Any contact with this equipment, or any contact above the waist, qualifies as a touch.

When one fencer scores a hit, the other fencer gets 170 milliseconds to respond before the scoring box locks them out. There are no épée-style double touches if both fencers score hits; instead, the right-of-way rules for sabre, which are significantly different from those for foil, are applied to determine who receives the touch.
